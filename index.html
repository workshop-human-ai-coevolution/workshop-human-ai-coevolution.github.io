Proposal

Human-AI Co-Evolution

Treating Human Evolution as a Goal in the Age of Agentic AI

NeurIPS 2026 · December 11-12, 2026 · Sydney, Australia

As AI gains execution authority, what should humans become, and how do we build AI that helps them get there?

About The Workshop

Over the past two years, humans can increasingly delegate more complex work to AI, and our roles change as AI gains more execution authority. Coding agents make this especially visible: developers went from chatbots that explain code, to copilots that complete it, to agents that implement whole tasks, and now to managed systems that plan and coordinate workflows. Similar shifts are emerging in science, driving, and law, with humans moving from direct execution to output verification, workflow supervision, and eventually system-level governance. To use evolving AI well and take on these changing roles, humans must also evolve the corresponding skills.

Most research on human-AI co-evolution still remains AI-centric: one line studies AI that improves itself while pushing humans out of the loop; another treats humans mainly as a source of data and feedback for better AI. Recent ICLR 2025 workshops on Human-AI Coevolution and Bidirectional Human-AI Alignment began to draw attention to reciprocal adaptation, but the rapid shift toward agentic AI makes the problem more urgent. This workshop takes that overlooked stance: human evolution must be treated as an explicit goal of human-AI co-evolution, not merely a means to build more powerful AI.

This goal matters for two reasons. First, humans cannot be assumed to use stronger AI effectively by default: as AI takes on more of the work, people have fewer chances to practice the judgment, oversight, and control they need, echoing long-standing concerns about automation bias, complacency, and deskilling. Second, human evolution benefits both sides of the loop: stronger human capability helps people use AI more effectively, and higher-quality feedback and process-level oversight provide better learning signals for future models. We bring together researchers across ML, RL, NLP, HCI, the learning sciences, and AI safety, building on the founding HAIC @ ICLR 2025 and Bi-Align workshops and the team's 1st Multi-Turn Interaction Workshop @ NeurIPS 2025.



Topics

We organize the workshop around a lifecycle of human evolution in agentic AI. Topics include but are not limited to:

(1) Evaluation of Human Capability Change:

Measuring how AI use empowers or erodes human skills, including independent thinking, domain expertise, calibration, vigilance, and the ability to work without AI.

(2) Human-Evolution-Centered AI Interaction:

Designing AI interfaces and interaction patterns that keep humans engaged, reflective, and in control rather than turning them into passive approvers.

(3) Scalable Oversight for Agentic AI:

Helping humans monitor longer workflows, catch failures, interrupt or redirect agents, and audit behavior when checking every step by hand no longer scales.

(4) Human Feedback for Better AI:

Studying how capable humans provide better feedback, corrections, demonstrations, and process-level supervision, and how weak feedback can make future models worse.

(5) Societal Conditions for Human Evolution:

Understanding how education, workplaces, policy, liability, and labor structures shape whether people can practice and retain the skills needed to use AI well.



Call For Papers

The Workshop on Human-AI Co-Evolution (NeurIPS 2026) invites position papers, empirical studies, systems and benchmarks, and provocations on making human evolution a goal of human-AI co-evolution. We especially encourage work grounded in domains where the shift is already underway: software engineering, science, medicine, law, driving, and education. Submissions must present original work that has not appeared at an archival venue.

Submission Site

Submissions are non-archival and managed via OpenReview, remaining private during review. All authors should maintain up-to-date OpenReview profiles for conflict-of-interest management and paper matching. The submission portal will be announced once the workshop is accepted.

Scope

We welcome contributions across the five themes above. Accepted papers are presented as posters, with a subset selected for oral or spotlight talks. The workshop is in person at NeurIPS 2026 in Sydney.

Submission Guidelines

Tracks & Formatting
Submissions must be in English and use the NeurIPS 2026 workshop LaTeX template, submitted as a single PDF:
  • Full Papers: at most 9 pages (main text)
  • Short Papers: at most 4 pages (main text)
  • Both tracks welcome position papers, empirical studies, systems, benchmarks, and provocations.
  • References and appendices do not count toward the page limit, but the main text must be self-contained.
Review
The workshop uses double-blind review. Submissions must be anonymized, with author names, affiliations, and acknowledgments removed and prior work cited in the third person. Every submission receives at least three reviews via OpenReview, with conflicts managed through automated detection.

Non-Archival Policy

The workshop is non-archival. Papers under review elsewhere are welcome, and accepted papers may be published at other venues afterward. Work already published at NeurIPS or other archival venues should not be submitted.
