Proposal

Human-AI Co-Evolution

Treating Human Evolution as a Goal in the Age of Agentic AI

NeurIPS 2026 · December 11-12, 2026 · Sydney, Australia

As AI gains execution authority, what should humans become, and how do we build AI that helps them get there?

About The Workshop

AI no longer only answers questions. It drafts artifacts, runs end-to-end workflows, and coordinates other agents on our behalf. As people delegate more, their role shifts from doing the work to judging, verifying, supervising, and governing it. But the very capabilities this demands, such as critical thinking, evaluative expertise, metacognitive monitoring, and systems thinking, fade with disuse. This is Bainbridge's irony of automation, and the evidence is mounting: people over-trust AI, miss its errors, and rate flawed outputs as better than they are.

Most research on human-AI co-evolution stays AI-centric: improving AI with humans out of the loop, or treating humans as a route to better models. This workshop takes the opposite stance: human evolution should be an explicit goal, not merely a means to stronger models. It is not zero-sum: when people evolve the skills to use AI well, they use it more effectively and give better feedback that improves the next model. We convene researchers across ML, RL, NLP, HCI, the learning sciences, and AI safety, building on the founding HAIC @ ICLR 2025 and the team's 1st Multi-Turn Interaction Workshop @ NeurIPS 2025.

A Four-Phase Framework

We organize the question by how much execution authority humans hand to AI. As AI advances from Tool to Assistant to Executor to Organization, it weakens a specific human capability. Each phase names the skill to sustain and the AI-side support that helps, and the same framework doubles as our research agenda.

1 · Tool
Returns answers.
⚠ Weakened reasoning
🎯 Sustain: critical thinking
🛠 AI support: reasoning scaffolds
2 · Assistant
Produces partial artifacts.
⚠ Missed flaws
🎯 Sustain: evaluative expertise
🛠 AI support: verifiable artifacts
3 · Executor
Runs autonomous workflows.
⚠ Undetected drift
🎯 Sustain: metacognitive monitoring
🛠 AI support: interruptible workflows
4 · Organization
Coordinates systems of agents.
⚠ Lost goal traceability
🎯 Sustain: systems thinking
🛠 AI support: auditable systems


Topics

We organize submissions around five themes that map onto the framework above. Topics include but are not limited to:

(1) Evidence and Benchmarks for Human Capability Change:

AI use does not change human capability in one direction: the same tool that speeds knowledge access can hollow out reasoning through disuse. We invite work that builds the evidence base for this two-sided effect: methods to detect deskilling, cognitive offloading, automation bias, and miscalibration; longitudinal and AI-off study designs that separate genuine capability from AI-propped performance; and measurement that scales with AI autonomy.

(2) Human-Evolution-Centered AI Design:

If AI can erode human capability, it can also be built to sustain it. We seek interaction and system design that keeps humans sharp rather than passive: reasoning scaffolds and cognitive forcing functions; artifacts that expose assumptions and likely failure modes instead of only a polished result; agents that surface progress and pause at low confidence; and intent-preserving audit trails for multi-agent systems.

(3) From Oversight to Governance Across Levels of Autonomy:

As humans move from doing to verifying to supervising to governing, checking everything by hand stops scaling. We invite work on scalable oversight at each phase: grounded verification of AI artifacts; intervention and takeover when workflows drift; sampling-based auditing of agent trajectories; AI-assisted oversight and its failure modes; and governance of multi-agent organizations through constitutional constraints and risk thresholds.

(4) Co-Evolution Feedback Loops Between Humans and AI Development:

Human evolution helps humans, and it also produces the signal AI learns from. When people approve outputs without thinking, reward goes to agreement, length, and formatting instead of truth, and models drift toward sycophancy and mode collapse. We connect human capability to model development: how better judgment yields better data; bidirectional human-AI alignment; the risks of training on AI-generated or low-effort feedback; and evaluation that rewards correctness over polish.

(5) Scaling Human Evolution across Education, Work, and Society:

Individual practice is not enough; the conditions for oversight must be built into institutions. We welcome work on AI literacy reframed as a capability progression rather than a static checklist; curricula that pair AI-assisted output with AI-off explanations; organizational practices such as review pipelines, checkpoints, and audits; and policy questions including liability, regulation that preserves auditability, and labor-market dynamics.



Call For Papers

The Workshop on Human-AI Co-Evolution (NeurIPS 2026) invites position papers, empirical studies, systems and benchmarks, and provocations on making human evolution a goal of human-AI co-evolution. We especially encourage work grounded in domains where the shift is already underway: software engineering, science, medicine, law, driving, and education. Submissions must present original work that has not appeared at an archival venue.

Submission Site

Submissions are non-archival and managed via OpenReview, remaining private during review. All authors should maintain up-to-date OpenReview profiles for conflict-of-interest management and paper matching. The submission portal will be announced once the workshop is accepted.

Scope

We welcome contributions across the five themes above. Accepted papers are presented as posters, with a subset selected for oral or spotlight talks. The workshop is in person at NeurIPS 2026 in Sydney.

Submission Guidelines

Tracks & Formatting
Submissions must be in English and use the NeurIPS 2026 workshop LaTeX template, submitted as a single PDF:
  • Full Papers: at most 8 pages (main text)
  • Short Papers: at most 4 pages (main text)
  • Tiny Papers: lower the barrier for under-resourced and early-career researchers, welcoming fresh perspectives, small studies, and provocations.
  • References and appendices do not count toward the page limit, but the main text must be self-contained.
Review
The workshop uses double-blind review. Submissions must be anonymized, with author names, affiliations, and acknowledgments removed and prior work cited in the third person. Every submission receives at least three reviews via OpenReview, with conflicts managed through automated detection.

Non-Archival Policy

The workshop is non-archival. Papers under review elsewhere are welcome, and accepted papers may be published at other venues afterward. Work already published at NeurIPS or other archival venues should not be submitted.
